Defibrillator

A defibrillator is a medical device used to restore normal heart rhythm in patients who are experiencing cardiac arrest or life-threatening arrhythmias. The device delivers an electrical shock to the heart, which helps it to resume a normal rhythm and function.

How it Works

A defibrillator usually consists of a computerized unit and two pads with electrodes that are placed on the patient's chest. The unit analyzes the patient's heart rhythm and, if necessary, delivers an electrical shock through the pads to restore a normal rhythm. Automated external defibrillators (AEDs) are portable devices that can be used by non-medical personnel with basic training to deliver a shock to a person experiencing sudden cardiac arrest.

Types of Defibrillators

There are several types of defibrillators, including external defibrillators, implanted defibrillators, and wearable defibrillators. External defibrillators are used in emergency situations where quick action is necessary. Implanted defibrillators are surgically implanted under the patient's skin and are used to monitor and regulate the heart's rhythm on a long-term basis. Wearable defibrillators are portable devices that are worn by patients who are at risk of sudden cardiac arrest, such as those with a history of heart disease or heart surgery.

Benefits of Defibrillators

Defibrillators can save lives by quickly restoring normal heart rhythm in patients who are experiencing cardiac arrest or life-threatening arrhythmias. They are easy to use and can be used by anyone with basic training, such as emergency responders, healthcare professionals, and even bystanders. Implantable defibrillators can also provide continuous monitoring and regulation of the heart's rhythm, reducing the risk of sudden cardiac death in high-risk patients.
