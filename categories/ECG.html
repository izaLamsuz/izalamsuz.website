Electrocardiograph-

Is the instrument that records the electrical activity of the heart Electrocardiogram (ECG) is the record of that activity.
  • As the heart undergoes depolarization and repolarization, the electrical currents that are generated spread not only within the heart,but also throughout the body.
  • This electrical activity generated by the heart can be measured by an array of electrodes placed on the body surface. The recorded tracing is called an electrocardiogram (ECG, or EKG). A ”typical” ECG tracing is shown to the right. The different waves that comprise the ECG represent the sequence of depolarization and repolarization of the atria and ventricles. The ECG is recorded at a speed of 25mm/sec (5 large squares/sec), and the voltages are calibrated so that 1 mV = 10 mm (2 large squares) in the vertical direction. Therefore, each small 1-mm square represents 0.04 sec (40 msec) in time and 0.10 mV in voltage. Because the recording speed is standardized, one can calculate the heart rate from the intervals between different waves.

    • ECG (Electrocardiogram)

    An electrocardiogram (ECG) is a medical test that is used to measure the electrical activity of the heart. It is a non-invasive test that is commonly used to diagnose heart conditions such as arrhythmias, heart attacks, and heart disease.

    How it Works?

    During an ECG, a healthcare professional attaches electrodes to the patient's chest, arms, and legs. These electrodes are connected to a machine that records the electrical activity of the heart, and it produces a graph called an electrocardiograph. The electrocardiograph displays the electrical impulses of the heart as waves that are interpreted by the healthcare professional.

    Types of ECG

    There are different types of ECG, including:

    • Resting ECG: This is the most common type of ECG, and it is done while the patient is lying still. It typically takes about 5-10 minutes to complete.
    • Exercise ECG: This type of ECG is done while the patient is exercising on a treadmill or stationary bike. It helps to evaluate the heart's response to physical activity.
    • Holter Monitor ECG: This type of ECG is a portable device that is worn by the patient for 24-48 hours. It records the heart's electrical activity during daily activities, allowing for an accurate diagnosis of arrhythmias and other heart conditions.

    Benefits of ECG

    An electrocardiogram provides several benefits, including:

    • Early detection of heart conditions, allowing for early intervention and treatment.
    • Non-invasive and painless procedure.
    • Quick and accurate diagnosis of heart conditions.
    • Allows healthcare providers to monitor the effectiveness of treatment or medications.
    • Helps to assess the risk of heart disease and evaluate overall heart health.

    Conclusion

    An electrocardiogram (ECG) is an essential medical test that helps to diagnose and monitor various heart conditions. It is a non-invasive and painless procedure, providing accurate and quick data that allows healthcare providers to identify and treat heart conditions early, leading to better patient outcomes. Different types of ECG are available, including the resting ECG, exercise ECG, and Holter monitor ECG. ECGs are an essential tool for evaluating a patient's overall heart health and ensuring early detection and treatment of heart disease.
